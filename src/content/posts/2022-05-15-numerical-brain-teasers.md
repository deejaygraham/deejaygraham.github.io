I was given the chance to have advance access to Erica Sadun's book "Numerical Brain Teasers" for technical review. Here are my thoughts on the early edition.

In general I didn’t find any of the puzzles were incorrect. A few of them totally stumped me but I think that was more that I didn’t find the type of 
puzzle interesting and after a working day (maybe the time I would reach for a book like this) my brain couldn’t cope with the extra mental power needed. 

The first chapter, number combinations was clearly explained and I appreciated the strategy guide at the beginning but a lot of the puzzles seemed to 
defeat me. Some I was able to do and looking at the solutions, I still wouldn’t have thought of some of them.

I found the next chapter more suited to me and was able to work through those quickly. I thought I had found a couple of issues with two of the 
grids (2 and 8) but it was my mental arithmetic that was wrong. 

In wandering digits the puzzles could do with more spacing like the example at the start of the chapter (or like Balanced Groups). I found I was 
looking up the answers and getting confused because I was reading Puzzle 12 and associating the numbers below it (actually Puzzle 13) so looking up 
the solution made me question the maths again :)

Skips was hugely confusing to begin with, when I followed the green how to play box and looked at the first example. This needed a longer explanation 
and the worked example that was provided, otherwise it would have been too abstract for me to try I think. Even working through the example, it 
took me a while to notice the rule at the bottom of the green box to use only numbers 1..4. After a bit of time absorbing the rules, I found 
these to be very enjoyable.

Balanced groups took a while to complete and felt more like work than any of the other puzzles

Magic Triangles, the solving the example section, I was confused by why you would use values close to the outer corners of the bottom row specifically 
and “balancing the sides” took me a while to understand (thinking about the sides as a completed thing, not that the 44 + 24 balance with 40 and s8). Once 
I got what the author was saying, I completed these quite quickly and enjoyed them.

Understanding the triangle chapter, squares were easier. Would have liked to hear more about the image processing involving magic squares as an 
aside in this chapter.

Group grids seemed more complex at the beginning of the chapter but turned out to be easy. In the description, saying that groups connect across 
sides made me think that opposite sides of the square could be used - e.g. the sixes on the left of the square could “wrap around” with the sixes on the 
right of the square to form a group (with other numbers in the middle). The rule about same size groups can’t touch made for confusion (again!) because 
in the example the two 6 groups are touching but diagonally. Once I was used to what the rules actually were rather than what I thought they were, 
these were much easier to figure out.

With water pails, I found the state graph explanation much easier to understand (never seen that described that way before) and was good to show how 
the number of possibilities was determined by drawing it out that way. It does take some thinking about but once it’s been shown it seems to be quite 
intuitive,  I like this approach and don’t think it takes anything away from solving the puzzles. At some points I was considering writing some python 
code to solve some of the puzzles earlier in the book!

Digit sum squares and Bit puzzles were probably the most fun for me to do, requiring the least mental arithmetic, not really sure why that was, maybe the 
visual layout of the puzzle being a bit more like soduku than the plain maths from earlier tricking my brain :)
With the digit sums I found a couple of alternatives but again, multiple solutions were allowed, so no problem there. 

For matchsticks I don’t think the author mentions anything about multiple solutions but the example has one - I initially thought I had got it 
wrong : take I from right hand side of equation to make plus in the same way 4 + 2 = 6

Bit puzzles were a nice way to round off the book, very like the old windows minesweeper game, using adjacent numbers to work out what was in the 
surrounding area and where the mines were.
